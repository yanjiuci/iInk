Fu: A Single Character Holding Millennia of Blessings, Inheriting Auspiciousness Through Brush and Ink

Fu is the warmest and most auspicious Chinese character in the hearts of the Chinese people. After more than four thousand years, it still carries people’s aspirations for happiness, health, peace, and fulfillment. Its origin embodies the devout prayers of ancient ancestors, and it has taken on countless graceful forms in the works of famous calligraphers through the ages, becoming a cultural symbol spanning time and space.

More than four thousand years ago, the character fu already depicted the ideal form of happiness. In ancient times, when agriculture was underdeveloped, grain was scarce, and using rice to brew wine was an extraordinary luxury. The rising aroma of wine and high flames were regarded as a heavenly omen. Therefore, the earliest form of fu resembled a pointed-bottom ritual vessel filled with wine. Its original meaning was that ancient people offered fine wine on the altar to pray for divine protection, which is the most primitive cultural origin of fu.

In the Eastern Han Dynasty, Xu Shen defined fu as "divine protection" in Explaining Simple and Analyzing Compound Characters, noting that it consists of the component shi (altar/deity) and the phonetic component fu. Later paleographers confirmed that the original form of fu was a wine-filled ritual vessel, clearly verifying its origin: offering wine to the gods to beg for blessings—the core logic of Chinese fu culture. From ancient sacrificial prayers to the later "Five Blessings" (longevity, wealth, health, virtue, and a peaceful end), the meaning of fu has been continuously enriched, becoming the ultimate vision of a better life for the Chinese people.

In the millennia-long inheritance of calligraphy, famous masters have endowed the character fu with unique styles, and each brushstroke bears the mark of the times, keeping this auspicious character alive in ink and brush.

The character fu written by Wang Xizhi, the "Sage of Calligraphy" of the Eastern Jin Dynasty, is elegant, smooth, and free-spirited. Huairen, a distinguished monk of the Tang Dynasty, spent 24 years collecting Wang Xizhi’s calligraphy to carve the Preface to the Holy Teachings, which perfectly preserved this classic fu. It has become a "living dictionary" of Wang Xizhi’s running script, showing the unrestrained elegance of Wei and Jin calligraphy.

In Yan Zhenqing’s early masterpiece Stele of the Multi-Treasure Pagoda (Tang Dynasty), the character fu features a rigorous structure and powerful strokes, laying the foundation for the solemn and majestic Yan-style regular script. For thousands of years, it has been a standard model for beginner calligraphy learners, displaying integrity and dignity in every stroke.

To promote calligraphy education, Zhao Mengfu of the Yuan Dynasty created Thousand Character Classic in Regular and Cursive Scripts, pairing standard regular script with lively cursive script for easy learning. The character fu in the phrase "Fu Yuan Shan Qing" (Blessings stem from kindness and virtue) is gentle, elegant, and calm, fully reflecting the quiet and upright spirit of Yuan Dynasty literati.

Deng Shiru, a great calligrapher of the Qing Dynasty, broke the shackles of traditional clerical script and integrated seal script techniques into clerical script. He transformed flat, rigid clerical script into round, vigorous strokes with a strong stone inscription flavor. His fu character is simple and majestic, ushering in a new era of prosperity for the stele studies movement in the Qing Dynasty and injecting new vitality into the clerical-script fu.

The "World’s No.1 Blessing", personally written by Emperor Kangxi of the Qing Dynasty, is a legendary treasure of fu culture. Emperor Kangxi wrote it to pray for his grandmother Empress Dowager Xiaozhuang. The character ingeniously contains five auspicious meanings: many sons, great talent, ample land, long life, and abundant blessings, so it is known as the "Longevity Blessing". The stone stele was missing for nearly a century and was unexpectedly unearthed at Prince Gong’s Mansion in Beijing in 1962, becoming a precious cultural relic.

From the pictograph of a wine vessel in oracle bone inscriptions to the textual interpretation in Explaining Simple and Analyzing Compound Characters; from Wang Xizhi’s elegant brushwork to the auspicious connotation of Emperor Kangxi’s imperial writing—the character fu has traveled through four thousand years. It is no longer just a Chinese character, but a cultural belief rooted in the blood of the Chinese people. Through brush and ink, it carries the timeless good wishes of generations and shines brightly through history.
